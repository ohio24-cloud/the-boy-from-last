Chapter One

The Window Seat

"Some stories begin in silence — with a boy near a window, and a professor who notices him too late."

✦1988 / Madrid

✦Psychological Fiction

✦Observation / Secrets / Obsession

I

The winter of 1988 had arrived in Madrid with a hard, bitter cold that made even the streetlights look lonely. That night, the city glowed beneath a pale haze of frost, and the literary world had gathered for the launch of a new novel by Pedro — a writer already famous, already admired, already spoken of with that strange mixture of reverence and envy that follows successful men.

Among the crowd stood Professor Jenn, younger then, carrying in his chest the restlessness of a man who wanted to become more than he was. He too wrote stories, though his pages remained hidden, unfinished, and uncertain. Pedro, to Jenn, was not merely a novelist — he was proof that stories could become power, that words could bend reality itself.

When the launch ended and people began to scatter into the freezing night, Jenn gathered enough courage to stop him. He asked the question that had lived in him for years: How do I become a better writer?

Pedro barely slowed. He lit a cigarette, the orange glow briefly cutting through the dark, and exhaled with visible impatience. "Think about your own story," he said, almost casually, as if brushing dust from his sleeve. "I'm in a hurry. I have dinner with Mister Kang."

And then he walked away — smoke trailing behind him, his figure disappearing into the cold Madrid night — leaving Jenn with nothing but the sting of dismissal and the echo of a sentence that would stay with him far longer than either of them could have imagined.

II

Ninteen Years later, Jenn had become a literature professor at Madrid High School. His classroom was orderly, quiet, and ruled by his belief that stories revealed more about a person than any confession ever could. He gave his students a simple but relentless assignment: write every day, write honestly, and above all — do not cheat.

One afternoon, while the winter light leaned weakly through the classroom windows, a phone suddenly rang in the middle of class. The sharp sound split the silence. Jenn stopped mid-sentence and fixed his gaze on the student responsible. The boy rose awkwardly, already embarrassed, but Jenn did not let him off so easily.

Instead, he began questioning him about One Hundred Years of Solitude. The student stumbled. He gave the writer's name incorrectly, then failed to explain the novel itself. Jenn's expression hardened. He told him to stand outside the classroom.

Near the window sat Floyy — quiet, detached, almost invisible in the back row. He had listened to everything. And just as the silence settled, he spoke.

Calmly, almost too calmly, Floyy corrected Jenn himself. He gave the right writer's name and pointed out that the professor had said it wrong. The room froze.

Jenn stared at him, shocked less by the correction than by the audacity of it. "How dare you speak to a professor like that?" he snapped. But before the tension could grow any sharper, the bell rang. The class broke apart at once, chairs scraping, bags lifted, voices returning.

Outside the classroom, the boy whose phone had rung approached Floyy with visible relief. "Thanks," he said. "You saved me today." Then, with the easy confidence of someone who had never learned to hide his feelings, he added, "Lunch is on me. Tell me what you want."

His name was Peter.

III

The next day, Peter chose not to sit in his usual place. Instead, he dropped into the seat beside Floyy near the window, as though the decision had already been made somewhere long before either of them arrived in class.

Floyy, though quiet, had a reputation for being unusually talented with computers. He was part of the college coding club, and Peter noticed the worn tote bag lying near his chair. That was enough to start a conversation.

"You know coding?" Peter asked. "I joined the coding club too. Can you teach me?"

Floyy didn't even look up properly. He said he had no time — that he worked part-time and couldn't waste hours teaching someone else. Peter, however, was persistent in the warm, almost reckless way only certain people are. He bargained with him, smiling, insisting that three days a week would be enough.

Somehow, after enough pressure and enough silence, Floyy agreed.

That evening, Peter took him home. The house itself was larger than anything Floyy was used to — polished floors, expensive quiet, and the kind of comfort that seemed to belong to another world entirely.

Peter's mother was in the garden room, tending to flowers with the care of someone who had learned to pour feeling into things that did not argue back. "You're back from college," she said when she saw him, her voice soft and effortless.

Peter introduced Floyy.

And in that single moment, something shifted.

Floyy looked at Merissa — really looked at her — and for a second the world around him seemed to blur. He became strangely still, almost spellbound, as if he had just stumbled into the beginning of a story far more dangerous than the one he thought he was living in.

Peter noticed nothing. Merissa only smiled politely. But somewhere behind Floyy's quiet face, the machinery of observation had already begun to turn.

The story has only just begun.
Some doors open quietly — and some obsessions begin with a single look.
