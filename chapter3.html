Chapter Three

The Conversation by the Stairs

"Some lines, once crossed, can never be uncrossed. And some requests, once made, change everything between two people forever."

✦Psychological Fiction

✦Manipulation / Crossing Lines / The Price of Stories

I

Floyy did not leave Peter's house immediately that evening.

At first, it was for no reason he could have explained. Peter had gone back upstairs to search for a coding notebook he claimed he had misplaced, and Floyy, already half-ready to leave, found himself standing alone in the hallway with his coat hanging loose from one shoulder. The house was quieter than usual, the kind of silence that comes only after a long day has finally folded into night.

Downstairs, a lamp had been switched on in the sitting room.

From where he stood near the staircase, Floyy could see only part of it — the edge of a cream sofa, the low glass table between it and the armchair opposite, and the soft amber light pooling over two cups of coffee. He could hear their voices more clearly than he could see their faces.

Merissa spoke first.

"There's something wrong with that boy."

Her voice was calm, but not casual. Not the voice of someone gossiping over coffee. It was the voice of someone trying very hard not to sound afraid.

Floyy stopped moving.

Peter's father gave a quiet laugh — not cruel, just dismissive, the laugh of a man who believed discomfort could be reasoned away if spoken to gently enough.

"You're imagining things," he said. "He's strange, maybe. Quiet. But strange doesn't mean dangerous."

Merissa did not answer immediately. Floyy could picture her then without even seeing her: one hand around the coffee cup, eyes lowered, expression distant in the way it became when she was thinking harder than she wanted anyone to notice.

"I found him in my room," she said at last.

Peter's father exhaled through his nose, as if he had already grown tired of the conversation before it had properly begun.

"And he told you he couldn't find the washroom."

"Pedro."

"What do you want me to say?" he replied, more softly now. "I've met the boy. He's helping Peter. Peter actually listens to him. You know how rare that is."

Merissa's tone sharpened, though only slightly. "That doesn't change what happened."

"No," Pedro said. "But it doesn't automatically mean what you think it means either."

The room went quiet for a few seconds. Floyy remained perfectly still near the staircase, one hand resting against the polished wooden rail, his breath so shallow it barely seemed to exist.

Then Merissa spoke again.

"I don't know," she said. "Maybe it's nothing. Maybe I'm making too much of it. But there's something about him I don't like. Something… off."

Floyy lowered his eyes.

Below, the faint clink of porcelain sounded against glass.

Pedro leaned back into his chair; Floyy could hear it in the movement of the fabric. When he spoke again, his tone had shifted into something practical, almost soothing.

"Peter's coding competition is close," he said. "Let that finish first. Once it's over, if you still feel the same way, we'll tell Peter he can't bring Floyy here anymore."

Merissa said nothing.

Pedro continued, as if the matter had already been settled by the sound of his own voice.

"Just until the competition is done," he said. "After that, if you want, we'll keep some distance."

Floyy's fingers tightened slightly around the stair rail.

He stayed there for another few seconds, listening to the silence that followed — not because he needed to hear more, but because he wanted to feel the sentence settle inside him completely.

After the competition.

We'll tell Peter he can't bring Floyy here anymore.

By the time Peter returned to the hallway, holding the missing notebook in one hand and apologizing for the delay, Floyy's face had already returned to normal.

He smiled faintly, said goodbye to Merissa and Pedro with the same politeness he always used, and stepped out into the winter night as if nothing at all had changed.

But by the time he reached home, he knew exactly what had.

II

The next evening, Professor Jenn's study was lit by the same yellow lamp as before, but the room felt smaller now, more watchful, as if it had not entirely forgotten the previous lesson.

Jenn sat behind the desk with Floyy's pages in his hand, reading the final paragraph again with visible irritation.

"That's it?" he asked, lifting the paper. "You stop here?"

Floyy sat opposite him in silence.

Jenn frowned and tapped the final line with one finger.

To be continued.....

"What is this supposed to mean?"

Floyy looked at the page, then at Jenn.

"It means," he said quietly, "that maybe there won't be any more."

Jenn's expression changed.

"What do you mean, there won't be any more?"

Floyy leaned back in his chair and folded his arms. His tone remained almost lazy, but there was something deliberate in the way he delivered each word — as if he had already rehearsed the effect they would have.

"I heard Peter's parents talking yesterday," he said. "They think I'm strange. Merissa doesn't want me in the house anymore."

Jenn went still.

Floyy watched him.

"They're waiting until after the coding competition," he continued. "Then they'll tell Peter not to bring me there again."

The paper in Jenn's hand lowered by an inch.

"You heard them say that?"

Floyy gave the smallest nod.

For a second Jenn did not speak. He only stared, trying to decide whether he was more disturbed by the information itself or by the calmness with which it had been delivered.

"And because of that," Jenn said slowly, "you're planning to stop these sessions?"

Floyy smiled, but it was the kind of smile that looked more like a private thought than a real expression.

"What would be the point?" he asked. "If I can't go to Peter's house anymore, then I won't have anything left to write."

Jenn's jaw tightened.

"That's not how writing works."

"Maybe not for you."

The room seemed to contract around them.

Jenn set the pages down on the desk with more force than necessary. "So that's it? You just give up?"

Floyy shrugged.

"I didn't say I wanted to."

"Then what are you saying?"

Floyy let the silence linger just long enough to become uncomfortable. Then he looked up and said, almost lightly:

"I'm saying Peter and I won't win that coding competition."

Jenn blinked.

"What?"

"We don't have enough time," Floyy said. "Not really. Peter's good, but not good enough. I'm good, but not good enough to drag both of us to first place in a few days. If we lose, then there's no reason for them to keep me around. Peter will go back to being their son, I'll go back to being the strange boy from school, and the door will close."

Jenn stared at him.

Floyy's voice softened.

"And if the door closes," he said, "the story ends."

Jenn hated the way that sentence sounded in the room.

He hated even more that part of him understood it.

"What exactly do you want from me?" he asked.

Floyy lowered his gaze, as if considering whether Jenn was ready to hear the answer.

Then he said:

"There might be one way."

Jenn felt something unpleasant move under his ribs.

"What way?"

Floyy looked up again.

This time the smile that touched his mouth was not shy, not uncertain, and not particularly human. It was the smile of someone setting a glass on the edge of a table just to see if the person across from him would notice before it fell.

"You'll have to do something for me," he said.

Jenn's voice turned flat. "What?"

Floyy spoke so quietly that, for a moment, the words almost didn't seem real.

"You'll have to steal something."

Jenn rose from his chair so abruptly that it scraped harshly against the wooden floor.

"What?"

Floyy did not move.

"A small thing," he said. "Not money. Not jewelry. Just… something."

Jenn stared at him as though the room itself had started speaking.

"You come into my house," Jenn snapped, "you sit in front of me, and you ask me — a professor — to steal?"

Floyy's expression didn't change.

Jenn's voice sharpened, louder now, almost shaking with disbelief.

"Get out."

Still Floyy remained seated.

"Did you hear me?"

Jenn stepped around the desk, pointing toward the door with a trembling hand.

"Get out of my house. Right now."

Only then did Floyy rise.

He picked up his bag slowly, almost politely, as if he were leaving at the end of an ordinary lesson instead of being thrown out of the room. When he reached the door, he paused with one hand on the handle and turned back.

Jenn was still standing beside the desk, breathing harder than he wanted to admit.

Floyy's eyes rested on him for a long moment.

Then he said, very softly:

"You haven't even asked why."

Jenn didn't answer.

Floyy smiled.

And with that, he opened the door and walked out, leaving Jenn alone in the yellow light of the study — furious, unsettled, and unable to stop wondering why, exactly, Floyy wanted him to steal something at all.

Stay Tuned

The story had changed shape now.
Floyy was no longer only watching from the edge of other people's lives.

He had begun to ask them to cross lines with him.
And Jenn, no matter how angry he was, had already made the first mistake:

He was still curious.
