A Special Interlude

The Season of Wren

"Hold that thought." She was already halfway to her feet. "You can finish telling me later."

✦Psychological Fiction

✦First Love / Silence / What Was Never Said

That year, in the Basque hills, autumn arrived before it was supposed to.

The leaves did not wait for the calendar. They simply let go — early, unhurried, as if the trees themselves had decided that this particular year deserved to end sooner.

Jenn was young then.

A literature student at the Ibarra School of Letters, the kind of boy who had loved books before he had loved anything else. Before he understood what love even meant.

That morning, he stood behind a tree.

Watching.

There was a girl sitting on the bench across the courtyard — a wide, weathered bench, and behind her, a tree already half-bare, its remaining leaves the color of old paper.

She looked, somehow, like she belonged to the season.

Jenn watched her for a long moment. Then, slowly, he stepped out from behind the tree and walked toward her.

"Hi, Wren."

She looked up. Smiled — the kind of smile that made the morning feel less cold.

"Jenn." A pause. "Didn't feel like going to class today either?"

"No."

That was all he said. He never had much more to say — not out loud, anyway.

He held out a book.

"This is for you, Wren."

She took it. Turned it over in her hands. And then her eyes widened, and something in her face lit up in a way Jenn would spend years trying to describe and never quite manage.

"Wait—" She looked up at him. "How did you know Elena Duarte is my favorite writer?"

Jenn only smiled.

He didn't answer.

Maybe because the answer was too simple — I know everything about you, Wren, because I have spent months paying attention to things you never noticed I was paying attention to — and some truths sound foolish once they're actually said aloud.

He opened his mouth. Something was finally about to come out — a sentence he had rehearsed, quietly, more times than he would ever admit.

"Wren!"

A voice, from across the courtyard. Her classmates, waving her over.

She turned toward them. Then back to Jenn, the book still held against her chest like something she wasn't ready to let go of.

"I have to go." She smiled once more. "Bye, Jenn."

And she left.

Just like that.

Jenn stood there, alone, beside the bench, beneath the half-bare tree, holding nothing but the shape of a sentence he never finished.

It rained, three days later.

Jenn had no umbrella. Neither, it turned out, did Wren — he found her standing beneath the library's stone awning, arms wrapped around herself, watching the courtyard disappear behind a curtain of water.

"You're going to be here a while," he said.

"So are you, apparently."

He shrugged off his jacket and held it out. She hesitated — just a second — then let him drape it over her shoulders. It was too big for her. She didn't seem to mind.

They stood there together, saying almost nothing, while the rain filled the silence for them.

"You always do this," she said eventually, glancing sideways at him.

"Do what?"

"Give me things." She pulled the jacket a little tighter. "Books. Umbrellas you don't have. What am I supposed to give you back?"

Jenn looked at her for a long moment. The rain had softened everything — her voice, the light, the careful distance he always kept between them.

Just stay, he thought. That would be enough.

"Nothing," he said instead. "I don't need anything back."

It was, he would realize years later, the closest he ever came to telling her the truth without actually saying it.

Some weeks after that, she found him in the library, buried behind a stack of books nearly as tall as he was.

"What is all this?"

"Research." He didn't look up. "For a paper."

She pulled out the chair across from him and sat down anyway, uninvited, the way she always did — as if she had never once considered that he might want to be alone.

He didn't mind. He never minded.

At some point, her hand reached for the same book his was reaching for. Their fingers brushed — barely, for less than a second — and both of them pulled back at the same moment, both suddenly very interested in anything else in the room.

"Sorry," she said.

"It's fine."

Neither of them moved to pick the book back up for a while.

He memorized that second. The way her fingers had felt, briefly, against his. He would think about it more times than he'd ever admit — turning it over in his mind the way she had once turned his book over in her hands, looking for something he never had the courage to place there on purpose.

The last night of autumn that year, the school lit a bonfire in the courtyard — a small tradition, more excuse for noise than anything else.

Jenn found Wren sitting apart from the others, close enough to the fire to catch its light, far enough to still feel like her own quiet corner of the world.

He sat beside her. Neither of them spoke for a while.

"I like this," she said finally, watching the flames. "The end of a season. Everyone always says it's sad. I think it's honest."

"Honest how?"

"It doesn't pretend to stay." She glanced at him. "It just... goes. No apology."

Jenn looked at the fire instead of at her, because looking at her, in that light, made it very hard to think clearly.

"Wren—"

"Hm?"

He had rehearsed this too, more than once, lying awake, choosing and discarding a hundred different first lines.

"There's something I've wanted to—"

"WREN, THEY'RE ABOUT TO DO THE LEAF TOSS — COME ON!"

Her friends, again. Always, somehow, at exactly the wrong second.

She laughed, already halfway to her feet. "Hold that thought." She held out her hand, pulling him up with her before he could protest. "Come toss leaves with us. You can finish telling me later."

Later came and went, the way it always did with them — folded quietly into some other day, some other conversation, until there were no more days left to fold it into.

He never did finish that sentence.

Not that night. Not any night after.

It became a pattern.

Morning after morning, he found small ways to be near her — a shared table at the library, a walk that happened to go the same direction, a book left where he knew she'd find it. And every time, he came close. Every time, something interrupted him — a friend calling her away, a bell ringing, a moment slipping past before he could catch it.

He told himself there would be another morning.

There always was, until — quietly, without any single dramatic goodbye — there wasn't.

Time did what time does. It moved. Jenn moved with it, carrying a version of that autumn with him that he never fully set down, even years later, even when he told himself he had.

He never told her.

Not once.

He wrote about her instead — buried her into every story that came after, disguised in different names, different faces, twelve different women who were somehow, quietly, always her.

He told himself it was enough.

It was never enough.

Rain fell over the city, the same rain that had been falling all week.

Jenn sat alone in his study, Floyy's assignment forgotten on the desk beside him, one name circled in his own handwriting.

Marissa.

He stared at it for a long time.

Marissa.

Wren.

"Her full name was... Wren Marissa."

The same person. The same laugh he had once tried, badly, to describe in a novel he swore was about twelve different women. The same bench, the same half-bare tree, the same sentence he had never managed to finish.

He hadn't known, back then, who she would become. Hadn't known that somewhere between that courtyard and now, she had married a man named Pedro — a man Jenn had once respected, once envied, once sat across from at lunch and called, almost, a friend.

A man who, it was becoming clearer by the hour, was not who anyone had believed him to be.

Jenn's hands tightened around the paper.

He thought of Wren on that bench, book pressed to her chest, asking how did you know — and he thought, for the first time in years, with a clarity that felt almost cruel:

Maybe if I had told her that morning... maybe none of this would have happened at all.

Maybe she would never have found her way to a man like Pedro.

Maybe I let her walk toward all of this... simply by staying silent.

Outside, the rain continued.

He whispered to himself, again, the same question that had followed him home from Hotel Bellavista.

"...What have I failed to see?"

But this time, he already knew.

He had failed to see her. Really see her. Not from behind a tree. Not from a careful, safe distance.

He had simply... watched.

And watching, he understood now, had never once been the same as loving someone out loud.
